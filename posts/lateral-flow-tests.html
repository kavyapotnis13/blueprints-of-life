Lateral Flow Tests, Reimagined

Various medical tests utilize urine due to its accessibility and the fact that it can be collected without needles. However, many important particles and molecules found in urine are far too diluted for simple rapid tests to detect. These tests, called lateral flow tests, such as pregnancy tests or COVID strip tests, require a certain amount of a molecule to produce a positive result. If the molecule is not present in sufficient quantities, it goes undetected, making it difficult to diagnose critical conditions ranging from early pregnancy to tuberculosis and serious infections.

To solve this problem, Samuel Chen et al. engineered a device known as an osmotic processor, whose job is to concentrate a urine sample so that important molecules become detectable and can be properly evaluated. The device utilizes osmosis, the process by which water naturally moves toward an area with a higher concentration of dissolved substances. Inside the osmotic processor, the urine sample resides in a separate compartment that is permeable only to water. Surrounding this compartment is a thick polymer solution that draws water outward, leaving behind the important molecules in a smaller amount of liquid and making them 100–200 times more concentrated than before.

Chen's team tested various polymers to determine which could extract water the fastest. They found that PEG, or polyethylene glycol, was the most effective, especially the highly concentrated variant PEG 1500. The device itself was manufactured using a 3D printer and designed to spread the sample into a thin layer, speeding up the water extraction process. Additionally, because the device operates without electricity, it is more accessible in areas that may not have reliable power sources.

To assess the device's effectiveness in real clinical testing, the researchers examined two examples: the hormone hCG, used in pregnancy tests, and the SARS-CoV-2 nucleocapsid protein, used in COVID tests. Before running the experiments, these molecules were diluted to the point of being undetectable. The test strips initially showed no sign of the molecules. After running the samples through the osmotic processor, however, the tests immediately indicated their presence. This level of concentration is critical because it allows hormones and other biomarkers to be detected earlier, enabling faster screening and treatment.

Other methods for concentrating samples often require machines, electricity, or expensive materials. What sets the osmotic processor apart is its ability to function independently without pumps, power, or other external energy sources. Low-resource areas, such as rural clinics, mobile testing sites, and disaster zones, could particularly benefit from this technology.

Overall, this device highlights the importance of equipment that functions independently while delivering rapid results. With further development, the osmotic processor may even be integrated directly with test strips, making it even easier to use. In a world where medical technology continues to advance at breakneck speeds, this innovation opens a promising path toward more effective, widely accessible diagnostics that allow people to receive care sooner and more successfully.
