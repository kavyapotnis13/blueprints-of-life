Carolyn Bertozzi

Imagine being able to watch chemistry happen inside a living cell in real time, without disturbing it. For Professor Carolyn Bertozzi, that imagination became a career, and that career became a field. Her work has fundamentally changed the way scientists understand the human body at a molecular level, and it started nowhere near a laboratory.

Bertozzi was drawn first to music, playing in bands through her teenage years with the same intensity she would later bring to research. Harvard University is where science began to pull harder. As an undergraduate researcher, she worked on a photoacoustic calorimeter and earned recognition for her experimental precision; a signal, in retrospect, of the methodical creativity that would come to define her. Her mentors helped her arrive at a conviction she has carried since: that chemistry is not just a discipline, but a language for understanding life.

Her PhD sharpened that conviction around a detail most researchers had treated as background noise. Studying how viruses interact with the body, she kept returning to sugars: specifically, the complex sugar molecules called glycans that coat the surface of human cells. Viruses, she found, could bind to these glycans. They were active participants in disease, and the tools to study them properly inside a living system simply didn't exist yet.

Bertozzi built those tools herself. She founded the field of bioorthogonal chemistry, coining the term to describe chemical reactions engineered to occur inside living organisms without interfering with surrounding biology. The idea was precise by design: synthetic molecules that react only with each other, invisible to the thousands of other molecules already present in a cell, allowing scientists to tag and track biological processes as they actually happen. It filled a gap the field had needed filled for decades. In 2022, that contribution earned her the Nobel Prize in Chemistry, shared with Morten Meldal and K. Barry Sharpless.

Armed with those tools, she turned back to sugars. Her lab mapped how glycans on cell surfaces shift in disease, particularly in cancer, where tumor cells wear unusual sugar coatings that help them hide from the immune system. Understanding how those coatings could be manipulated opened an entirely new angle on cancer treatment: not just targeting the tumor itself, but dismantling the molecular disguise protecting it. It reframed a whole category of therapeutic thinking.

That thinking has made it into the clinic. Bertozzi co-founded companies including Palleon Pharmaceuticals and Enable Biosciences, both grounded in the glycan biology her lab developed. Candidates built on the sugar-stripping approach are now in clinical trials; the arc she had long envisioned, from mechanism to molecule to medicine.

Carolyn Bertozzi didn't wait for better tools. She built them, used them to see something nobody had seen clearly before, and then translated what she saw into treatments for some of medicine's hardest problems. The field she founded is still young, and the pipeline is still growing, which suggests the most consequential chapters of her story may not yet be written.
