David Mooney

It's easy to imagine limb regeneration as a concept. We're all pretty familiar with it: superhero comics, Doctor Who, maybe even little worm documentaries we've seen on Nat Geo. But to imagine us regenerating? Outrageous.

David Mooney disagrees.

A professor at Harvard's Wyss Institute for Biologically Inspired Engineering, Mooney has spent decades working on a deceptively simple premise: the human body already knows how to heal itself, and the job of a biomaterials engineer is to give it better instructions. His lab doesn't build replacement parts so much as it builds scaffolds: materials designed to recruit the body's own cells, coax them into the right behavior, and then quietly step aside.

The foundation of that work is biomaterials. Mooney's lab engineers hydrogels and other soft materials that can be injected into damaged tissue and interact directly with the biological environment around them. These aren't inert implants. They're designed to be dynamic by releasing signals, responding to mechanical forces, degrading at a controlled rate as new tissue grows in to replace them. The material itself becomes part of the therapy.

One of the lab's most striking applications has been in wound healing and tissue regeneration. By loading biomaterial scaffolds with specific growth factors and immune-signaling molecules, Mooney's team has demonstrated that it's possible to direct the body's own stem cells to migrate into a wound site and rebuild damaged tissue without transplanting a single external cell. The body does the work. The material just tells it where to go.

That same logic has been extended to cancer immunotherapy. Rather than engineering immune cells outside the body and reinfusing them, Mooney's lab developed injectable scaffolds that train immune cells directly inside the body, recruiting and activating them at the site of a tumor. It's a fundamentally different philosophy: instead of removing the body from the equation, you make the body the reactor.

The clinical implications have been significant enough to attract serious commercial interest. Mooney co-founded Attivare Therapeutics and has been involved in other ventures translating the lab's biomaterial platforms toward human trials. The cancer immunotherapy scaffold approach, in particular, has drawn attention for its potential to make immunotherapy more accessible, cheaper to manufacture and simpler to administer than cell therapies that require complex personalization.

What makes Mooney's work difficult to neatly categorize is that it sits at the intersection of engineering, immunology, and cell biology simultaneously. He is not curing diseases so much as redesigning the conditions under which the body cures itself. That distinction matters, because it means his tools are, in principle, adaptable. The same scaffold logic that works for muscle regeneration can be rethought for bone, for nerve, for immune response.

The worms on Nat Geo regrow their tails through biology that evolution handed them for free. Mooney is working on something harder: building that capability back into a body that lost it. Piece by piece, material by material, he's making the outrageous look increasingly plausible.
