Jennifer Doudna

What would the world look like if we could edit the human genome? This is the fundamental question at the center of Professor Jennifer Doudna's career. Professor Doudna is perhaps best known for her contributions to the discovery and implementation of CRISPR-Cas9 genetic editing. Today, we'll talk about what led her to this discovery, her major contributions to the field, and what the future looks like for genetic medicine.

Growing up, Professor Doudna had always harbored an interest in science and mathematics. Her parents and teachers, she notes, were major supporters in her journey to become one of the leading contributors in modern science. Early in her career, she studied the three-dimensional structures of RNA to gain knowledge on its functions. Through this, she was able to learn about the information that was enclosed in the RNA enzyme, later using that knowledge to model CRISPR.

CRISPR is a gene-editing technology derived from the bacterial immune system. Explained simply, its purpose is to identify viral DNA, and forge RNA that is able to bind to the virus and stop it from replicating. This was first discovered by Japanese scientists in 1987, but Professor Doudna and colleague Emmanuelle Charpentier were the first to manipulate the technology to target specific strands of DNA using a specific gene within CRISPR, known as Cas9. With this, they found that CRISPR-Cas9 could cut specific genes out of plant and animal DNA, and even replace that with another.

After numerous tests, it was found that CRISPR-Cas9 had the ability to treat sickle cell disease, detect harmful mutations, as well as manipulate crop yield. Specifically, its ability to treat sickle cell disease heralded the first approved CRISPR-based therapy, an incredible milestone for the treatment of diseases. It had rewritten the rules of genetic expression; previously, gene therapies used to be slow, expensive and imprecise, but with CRISPR, it became stable and scalable. For their immense contribution to the field of genetics, Professors Doudna and Charpentier received a Nobel Prize, among other accolades. Their work set the foundation for many other scientists to identify and treat other diseases, founding a new branch in the field of genetics.

With all the advancement that has happened in the past, the world has one burning question: what's next? I imagine it would involve the development of increasingly effective treatments for various diseases. For example, there are many technologies currently in clinical trials: cancer therapies, inherited blindness, and many more. They all involve targeting the gene responsible for the harmful expression, and either muting it or replacing it with a harmless gene. This can be adapted across a wide range of illnesses and diseases, and I believe that as the field of gene therapies continues to gain traction, more therapies will be available for patients.

However, with more treatments comes greater discourse on the ethics of CRISPR and gene editing as a whole. Ethics was increasingly focused on after the 2018 "designer baby" controversy, where Chinese scientist He Jiankui was found altering the gene expression of embryos to present desirable traits in infants. Doudna herself has addressed some ethical concerns brought up by the greater scientific community. From a regulatory perspective, she highlights that the framework of the 1975 Asilomar Conference (a revolutionary conference defining the terms of usage for molecular technologies) largely overlaps with the needs of this technology. While she admits that additional regulations may be needed, she is confident that frameworks will evolve with the technologies that they govern.

Jennifer Doudna has answered the question: "What would the world look like if we could edit the human genome?" by creating the foundation for many technologies built to combat once-deadly diseases. CRISPR is a novel, futuristic technology that has the potential to solve many of today's problems, and with the right regulations, it may just become the backbone for the technology of the future.
