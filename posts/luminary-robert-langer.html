Robert Langer

The question Robert Langer has spent his career answering isn't glamorous, but it's fundamental: how do you get a drug to where it needs to go?

It sounds deceptively simple. But for decades, the answer to that question was: imprecisely, inefficiently, and often not at all. Langer changed that, and in doing so, helped launch a $100-billion-a-year industry and saved countless lives.

Graduating in the middle of the oil crisis of the mid-1970s, Langer could easily have taken a lucrative job with one of the big oil companies. Instead, he held out for a position in a medical setting, an unusual move for a chemical engineer, and one his peers were skeptical of. That skepticism would follow him for years. He was turned down nine times before securing funding for his own research, and when he applied for patents, he was repeatedly rejected. "People didn't believe in my ideas," he later said. "They didn't think this type of work was possible."

They were wrong. Working alongside cancer researcher Judah Folkman at Harvard, Langer was confronted with a problem that seemed almost intractable. Folkman needed a reliable way to deliver macromolecule-based drugs slowly and steadily over time, but such molecules are broken down by digestion and blocked by tissues if injected or inhaled. Langer's solution was to stop working with existing materials altogether. He custom-designed new polymers to release medication at a controlled rate, essentially building a biological clock into the drug's delivery system. The release could be tuned by the precise design and composition of the polymeric structure encasing the drug. It was a radical idea, and the scientific community largely said so.

But the results spoke for themselves. One early application was a biodegradable polymer wafer that could be surgically implanted at a tumor site to release an anti-cancer drug directly into the brain. From there, the applications multiplied. His inventions came to include drug release systems operated by remote control and a programmable implantable chip capable of dispensing precise drug doses on a set schedule. His lab also pioneered tissue engineering: 3-D synthetic polymer scaffolding on which skin, muscle, bone, and blood vessels can grow, with the long-term goal of producing fully implantable organs.

The cumulative scale of this work is difficult to overstate. Langer holds more than 800 patents and has published around 1,200 scientific papers. He has co-founded more than 40 companies, and technologies developed in his lab have benefited billions of patients worldwide. His co-founding of Moderna brought his work on nanoparticle drug delivery to global attention during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Where does the field go from here? Toward greater precision, smaller interventions, smarter systems. It could be in the form of implantable devices that monitor and respond to the body in real time, or nanoparticles engineered to seek out cancer cells specifically. It could even be controlled release systems precise enough to respond to glucose levels, a potential breakthrough for diabetes care. The trajectory is toward medicine that behaves less like a blunt instrument and more like a conversation between drug and body.

None of this is without complexity. Technologies this powerful raise serious questions about who has access to them, how they're regulated, and where the line sits between treatment and enhancement. Langer has consistently emphasized the role of public funding alongside private investment in making these innovations broadly available with a stance that reflects both his scientific values and his long memory of institutional resistance.

It took 28 years for the FDA to approve the first anti-cancer drug to come out of his work. That kind of timeline is a reminder that transformative science rarely moves at the speed its inventors would like. But it does move, and in Robert Langer's case, it has moved medicine into territory that once seemed like science fiction.
