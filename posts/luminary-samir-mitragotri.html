Samir Mitragotri

If you've ever wondered whether a shot could someday be replaced by a patch or a pill, there's a good chance the person working on that answer is Samir Mitragotri. He's one of the most influential bioengineers in the world today, and his career is a masterclass in what happens when rigorous science meets a genuine drive to help patients.

Mitragotri's path started in India, where he earned his bachelor's degree in chemical engineering from the Institute of Chemical Technology in Mumbai in 1992. From there, he headed to MIT, completing his PhD in chemical engineering in 1996. That combination of training set him up perfectly for the work he'd go on to do: applying hard engineering principles to soft, messy biological problems.

He spent over two decades at UC Santa Barbara, where he held the Duncan and Suzanne Mellichamp Chair and founded the university's Center for Bioengineering. In 2017, he moved to Harvard, where he now holds two named professorships: the Hiller Professorship of Bioengineering and the Hansjörg Wyss Professorship of Biologically Inspired Engineering, at the Harvard John A. Paulson School of Engineering and Applied Sciences and the Wyss Institute for Biologically Inspired Engineering.

Mitragotri's core obsession is drug delivery, specifically how to get medicine into the body without relying on needles. His lab has developed technologies that use ultrasound, ionic liquids, and skin patches to push proteins, peptides, and even small interfering RNA through the skin instead of through a syringe. He's also worked on oral delivery systems that could let patients take insulin and other peptide drugs as pills rather than injections, which would be a huge quality-of-life improvement for people managing chronic conditions like diabetes.

Beyond the skin and gut, his research digs into how the body's biological barriers work in general, and how engineered materials can be designed to work with those barriers rather than against them. That fundamental research has fed directly into applied technologies aimed at cancer, cardiovascular disease, multiple sclerosis, and infectious disease. A lot of his work doesn't stay in the lab either. He's known for pushing his discoveries toward actual clinical use, and several of his inventions have made it into human trials and real products.

The scale of his output is genuinely hard to overstate. He's authored more than 400 publications, and his work has been cited over 81,000 times, giving him an h-index of 140. He holds or has pending more than 225 patents. He's given over 500 invited lectures around the world. Few researchers in any field manage that kind of volume while also maintaining the kind of impact reflected in those citation numbers.

Mitragotri has been elected to essentially every major scientific academy that matters in his field. He's a member of the National Academy of Engineering, the National Academy of Medicine, and the National Academy of Inventors here in the US. Internationally, he's an elected member of the World Academy of Science, the American Academy of Arts and Sciences, and the Indian National Academy of Engineering. He's also a fellow of AAAS, AIMBE, and the Controlled Release Society, among other groups.

His early career recognition included being named one of MIT Technology Review's Innovators Under 35 back in 1999, and he's picked up field-specific honors since then, including the AIChE Allan P. Colburn Award and the Andreas Acrivos Award for Professional Progress.
